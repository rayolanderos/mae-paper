A PPD-Screening Journal for New Mothers Using Amazon Echo

EE382V-SE Project (Fall 2016)

Problem Definition

With regard to postpartum depression (PPD) and other perinatal mood and anxiety disorders, many mothers would rather rely on self-help methodology rather than seeking professional help for a number of reasons including cultural stigma, expectations for happiness during new motherhood, and distrust of formal mental health services.10 These barriers prevent women from identifying that they have PPD and receiving appropriate treatment as early as possible.

Early detection is important when identifying and treating PPD. According to the Postpartum Depression: Action Towards Causes and Treatment (PACT) Consortium,

“Clinical screening for depressive symptoms might occur only once in the postpartum period. A positive screen will be diagnosed as postpartum depression but will not delineate when symptoms began and the length of time for which they have been present. This lack of specificity could lead to diagnostic confusion and inadequate or ineffective treatment...Identification of whether the episode began before and continued into the pregnancy, during pregnancy, or in the postpartum period is, therefore, very important." - PPD ACT group information

Traditional methods for screening for PPD rely on standard questionnaires5 that new mothers often fill out with a primary care or even the new baby’s pediatrician during a routine well visit for herself or her child. In this context, a mother is less likely to self-identify as having any undesirable or challenging emotions or thoughts, as they are trying to focus on their own physical health or the health of their child.

In addition to the risks of PPD, new mothers are also at risk for feeling isolated and unsupported in general2. They also have the desire to share a lot of what they are experiencing throughout the day with loved ones, but may not feel that they have time to share their experiences.
